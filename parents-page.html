We know how much you care. Here is some more info.

Buying books that feature and promote diversity is a great first step. While the number of books featuring or written by Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC) has certainly increased in recent years, there is still much room for improvement.
According to the Cooperative Children’s Book Center, part of the School of Education, University of Wisconsin-Madison, only about 12% of children's books in 2019 prominantly included Black characters. There are more books with animals as main characters than there are of Black people.


One way to go a step further is to intentionally purchase books written by BIPOC authors or illustrated by BIPOC artists. Sales prove profitablity and drive publishers to print more books.


“In an ideal future, there will not only be more books about underrepresented people, but there will be more and more books written and illustrated by people representing their own diverse communities.”
- David Huyck, children’s book illustrator

When picking books, ask yourself a few questions.

  • Who tells the story?
  • Is it an authenic story from a first hand knowledge of the culture?
  • Are minority characters merely background characters?
  • Are there stereotypes or tropes used?
  • Is it a "white savior" narrative?
  • What larger conversation can come from this story?

Don't shy away from those hard conversations. The more we normalize these topics, the less taboo they become.
